## 1. Florida Building Code (Roofing)

### The 25% Rule (FBC Section 708.1.1)
> "Not more than 25 percent of the total roof area or roof section of any existing building or structure shall be repaired, replaced or recovered in any 12-month period unless the entire roofing system or roof section conforms to requirements of this code."

**When repair triggers full code upgrade:**
- If >25% of roof area or roof section is repaired/replaced/recovered within any 12-month period
- The 12-month window is cumulative: if 20% was replaced, and 6 months later another 10% needs repair, the rule is triggered (30% total)
- "Roof section" is defined by physical boundaries: expansion joints, parapets, or dividing walls create separate sections

**Critical 2007 Exception:**
- If the existing roof was built/repaired in compliance with the 2007 FBC or later editions, and 25%+ is being repaired, only the repaired portion must meet current code
- Pre-2007 roofs: entire system must be brought to current code when 25% threshold is crossed

**8th Edition FBC (2024-2025) Key Changes:**
- Two layers of underlayment required for: asphalt shingles, metal roof panels/shingles, mineral surfaced roll roofing, slate, and slate-type shingles (Section 1518.2.1)
- Stricter HVHZ (High-Velocity Hurricane Zone) fastening and uplift resistance standards for coastal counties
- Even minor storm damage can legally require full reroof under current code

### Permit Requirements
- Permits required for any roof replacement or significant repair affecting structural integrity, weatherproofing, or design
- Must be obtained BEFORE initiating work
- Application requires: detailed building plans (layout, materials, structural changes), safety features, code compliance documentation
- Proof of valid Florida roofing contractor license required
- **Consequences of unpermitted work:** fines, stop-work orders, failed inspections, mandatory tear-offs, denied insurance claims, delays in home sales

### Insurance Age-Based Rules (2025)
- Insurers allowed to deny or non-renew policies based entirely on roof age (15+ years), even if roof is in perfect condition

## 2. Wind Mitigation

### OIR Form 1802 (Uniform Mitigation Verification Inspection Form)
- Official form: OIR-B1-1802
- Valid for up to 5 years (unless material changes made or inaccuracies found)
- Updated version effective April 1, 2026 (based on 2024 Residential Wind-Loss Mitigation Study)
- Must be signed by a qualified inspector per Section 627.711(2), Florida Statutes
- At least one photograph required to validate each attribute (Questions 3-7)
- All documentation used to validate compliance must accompany the form
- Florida law requires all insurers to recognize and apply credits when verified

### Wind Mitigation Categories & Credits

#### 1. Roof Covering
- FBC-compliant roof covering vs. non-FBC
- Must meet current Florida Building Code wind resistance standards

#### 2. Roof Deck Attachment
| Level | Description | Discount |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Level A (Standard) | Plywood/OSB, 6d nails, 6"/6" spacing | Minimal |
| Level B (Intermediate) | Plywood/OSB, 8d common nails (min 2.25"), 6" OC edges / 12" OC field | Moderate |
| Level C (Superior) | Tongue & groove or dimensional lumber >= 3/4" thick with large nails (pre-1960 homes often qualify) | Maximum |

#### 3. Roof-to-Wall Connection
| Type | Description | Premium Reduction |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Toe Nails | Nails driven at angle through truss into top plate | None/minimal |
| Clips | Metal connectors on one side of truss | 25-35% wind premium |
| Single Wraps | Metal strap wrapping over truss, secured both sides | 25-35% wind premium |
| Double Wraps | Two single wraps or one connector with min 3 nails each side | Maximum discount |

**Retrofit costs:**
- Toenail to clip: starting $2,550+
- Toenail to single wrap: starting $3,550+
- My Safe Florida Home grants: up to $10,000 for roof-to-wall retrofits

#### 4. Roof Geometry
| Shape | Discount |
|-------|----------|
| Hip Roof (100% hip) | Maximum discount |
| Hip Combination (>90% hip perimeter) | Maximum discount |
| Other (>10% non-hip perimeter) | No geometry discount |

#### 5. Secondary Water Resistance (SWR)
- Self-adhered/self-sealing modified bitumen underlayment (peel & stick) over standard underlayment
- Modified bitumen waterproof membrane over code-approved underlayment
- Closed-cell foam adhesives coating underside of wood roof deck
- **This single feature can reduce wind premium by 30-50%**

#### 6. Opening Protection
- Impact-rated windows/shutters vs. non-rated
- Must cover all openings (windows, doors, garage doors)

## 3. IICRC Standards

### S500 - Water Damage Restoration (5th Edition, 2021)

#### Water Categories (Contamination Level)
| Category | Name | Description | Examples |
|----------|------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Clean Water | Sanitary source, no substantial risk | Broken supply lines, tub/sink overflow (no contaminants), appliance supply lines, melting ice/snow, rainwater, toilet tanks (not bowls) |
| 2 | Grey Water | Significant contamination, may cause discomfort/sickness | Dishwasher/washing machine discharge, toilet overflow (urine, no feces), hydrostatic seepage, aquariums, waterbeds |
| 3 | Black Water | Grossly contaminated, pathogenic/toxic agents | Sewage, rising floodwater, ground surface water intrusion, toilet overflow with feces |

**Category progression:** Clean water left untreated can degrade to Category 2 (within 24-48 hrs) and eventually Category 3.

#### Water Classes (Degree of Saturation)
| Class | Description | Drying Difficulty |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Limited intrusion, partial room, little/no carpet/pad | Easiest |
| 2 | Full room affected, wicked up walls <24 inches, carpet/pad soaked | Moderate |
| 3 | Multiple rooms, significant absorption, walls >24 inches, ceilings affected | Difficult |
| 4 | Bound/trapped water in materials (hardwood, vinyl-covered drywall, oil-painted walls) | Specialty equipment, longest drying |

#### Key Drying Requirements
- **Dry Standard:** Baseline moisture content (MC) readings from known dry materials in undamaged areas
- **Drying Goal:** Target moisture level = dry standard or within acceptable proximity
- **Documentation:** Comprehensive moisture documentation throughout: baseline conditions, progress, completion verification, evidence for insurance/liability
- **Equipment:** Standard calls for calculated equipment placement (dehumidifiers, air movers) based on class/category
- **Monitoring:** Regular moisture readings with meters (pin-type and pinless)

#### Core Procedure Areas
1. Psychrometry and Drying Technology
2. Equipment, Instruments, and Tools
3. Antimicrobial (biocide) Technology
4. Safety and Health (PPE requirements)
5. Administrative Procedures, Project Documentation, Risk Management

### S520 - Professional Mold Remediation (4th Edition, 2024)

#### Key Requirements
- **Source correction:** Must fix moisture source before remediation
- **Containment:** Proper physical containment barriers required
- **Pressure differentials:** Negative pressure in work area
- **HEPA filtration:** Required air filtration during remediation
- **PPE:** Appropriate personal protective equipment mandatory
- **Training:** Workers must be trained in mold remediation principles

#### Critical Rules
- Mold-resistant coatings MUST NOT be used as sealants/encapsulants over active viable mold growth
- Fungicidal coatings only effective AFTER visible growth removed, surface cleaned, and substrate dried
- Source removal is the standard of care - not encapsulation

### S700 - Fire and Smoke Damage Restoration (1st Edition, 2025)
> Note: S540 is for Trauma/Crime Scene Cleanup, NOT fire damage. S700 is the correct standard.

#### Scope Covers:
- Fire and Smoke Damage Restoration Contractor Qualifications
- Administrative Requirements and Documentation
- Fire and Smoke Damage (FSD) Assessment
- Fire Restoration Equipment, Tools, and Materials (ETM)
- Fire Restoration Mitigation and Source Removal
- HVAC and Air Conveyance Systems (ACS)
- Fire and Smoke Odor Management
- Fire and Smoke Damage Contents Restoration
- Post Restoration Evaluation
- Limitations, Complexities, Complications, and Conflicts

#### Key Concepts
- Restoration Work Plan (RWP) required
- Compliance with Authorities Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)
- Assessment of presence, intensity, and boundaries of fire residues/odors
- Practical principles for restoration cleaning and fire odor management

## 4. ISO Policy Forms

### HO-3 (Special Form - Homeowners)
**Most common homeowners policy in the US**

#### Coverage Structure
| Coverage | What It Covers | Basis |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| A - Dwelling | Home structure (interior + exterior) | Open peril (all risks unless excluded) |
| B - Other Structures | Detached garage, shed, fence | Open peril |
| C - Personal Property | Belongings, furniture, clothing | Named peril only |
| D - Loss of Use | Additional living expenses if displaced | Triggered by covered loss |
| E - Personal Liability | Lawsuits/claims against you | Per occurrence |
| F - Medical Payments | Injuries to others on property | Per person |

**Coverage Limits (typical % of Coverage A):**
- B: 10% of A
- C: 50% of A
- D: 20% of A

#### Standard Exclusions (HO-3)
- Flood (requires separate NFIP or private flood policy)
- Earthquake
- Mold/fungus/wet rot (unless caused by covered peril like burst pipe)
- Sewer/drain backup
- Neglect / lack of maintenance
- War, nuclear hazard
- Government action
- Intentional loss
- Earth movement

#### Personal Property Sublimits (common)
| Item Category | Typical Sublimit |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Jewelry, watches | $1,500 |
| Firearms | $2,500 |
| Silverware | $2,500 |
| Business property | $2,500 |
| Securities, deeds | $1,500 |
| Watercraft | $1,500 |
| Theft of jewelry | $1,500 |

### HO-6 (Condo Unit Owners Form)
**"Walls-in" coverage for condo owners**

#### Key Differences from HO-3
| Feature | HO-3 | HO-6 |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Dwelling coverage | Entire structure (ground up) | Interior only (walls-in) |
| Exterior/roof | Covered | Covered by condo association master policy |
| Common areas | N/A | Covered by condo association |
| Cost | Higher (covers more) | Lower |
| Lender requirement | Full rebuild cost | Walls-in coverage limits |

#### What HO-6 Covers
- Interior walls (drywall, paint, built-in fixtures)
- Flooring, cabinetry, appliances (owner-installed)
- Personal property
- Loss of use
- Personal liability
- Improvements/betterments to unit interior

#### What HO-6 Does NOT Cover
- Roof, exterior walls, outdoor areas
- Building structure, foundation
- Common areas, amenities
- These fall under the condo association's master policy

### DP-3 (Dwelling Property - Special Form)
**For non-owner-occupied properties (rentals, vacation homes)**

#### Key Differences from HO-3
| Feature | HO-3 | DP-3 |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Intended use | Owner-occupied | Non-owner-occupied (rentals) |
| Dwelling coverage | Open peril | Open peril (same) |
| Personal property | Included (named peril) | NOT included (endorsement needed) |
| Off-premises property | 100% of allowance worldwide | Limited to 10% |
| Theft coverage | Included | NOT included (endorsement available) |
| Liability | Included | NOT included (endorsement needed) |
| Coverage D | Additional living expense | Fair Rental Value (lost rental income) |

### HO-1 (Basic Form)
**Most limited coverage - largely discontinued**

#### Named Perils (only 10)
1. Fire or smoke
2. Explosions
3. Lightning
4. Hail and windstorms
5. Theft
6. Vandalism
7. Damage from vehicles
8. Damage from aircraft
9. Riots and civil commotion
10. Volcanic eruption

- Named peril only for BOTH dwelling and personal property
- Largely discontinued in most states due to limited coverage
- Only slightly cheaper than broader HO-3 forms

## 5. Depreciation

### How Carriers Calculate Depreciation
- **Formula:** Replacement Cost Value (RCV) - Depreciation = Actual Cash Value (ACV)
- **Method:** Estimate item's useful lifetime, reduce value by fraction each year
- **Example:** $10,000 roof with 20-year life = $500/year depreciation. At year 5: ACV = $7,500, depreciation = $2,500

### Recoverable vs. Non-Recoverable Depreciation

| Type | Policy Type | Can Recover? | How |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Recoverable | RCV (Replacement Cost Value) | Yes | Complete repairs, submit receipts/proof |
| Non-Recoverable | ACV (Actual Cash Value) | No | Depreciated amount is permanent loss |

### The Holdback System
1. **Initial payment:** Carrier pays ACV (holds back depreciation)
2. **Policyholder completes repairs** and provides proof of expenditure
3. **Supplemental payment:** Carrier releases recoverable depreciation (difference between ACV and RCV)
4. **Deadline:** Most policies require repairs within specific timeframe to recover depreciation

### Florida-Specific: Labor Depreciation
**Florida law is clear: labor is NOT depreciated in calculating ACV.**

- Leading case: **Trinidad v. Florida Peninsula Insurance Co.**, 121 So.3d 433 (Fla. 2013)
- Florida Supreme Court: "overhead and profit are like all other costs of a repair, such as labor and materials, the insured is reasonably likely to incur"
- Rationale: labor does not become obsolete over time, so depreciation factor = zero
- Policyholder entitled to ACV of insured loss (both labor and materials) even if policyholder performs repairs themselves
- **Common carrier error:** Depreciating labor component - this is improper under Florida law

### Common Depreciation Errors by Carriers
1. Depreciating labor costs (improper in FL)
2. Using incorrect useful life estimates
3. Not accounting for maintenance/condition
4. Applying blanket depreciation rates without item-specific analysis
5. Failing to release recoverable depreciation after proof of repairs submitted

### RSMeans Data
- Over 85,000 unit prices, 25,000 building assemblies, 42,000 repair/remodel costs
- Location-adjustable with city cost indexes for FL regions
- Subscription-based: RSMeans Data Online or annual cost books
- 2025 edition available (updated annually)
- Considered industry benchmark for fair market value pricing

### Xactimate Pricing Issues

#### Known Problems
1. **Regional inconsistencies:** Adjacent FL markets (e.g., Winter Haven vs. Orlando) show $84.81/square price difference = $3,562.02 variance on a 42-square roof
2. **Price deflation:** Pre-installed prices have been steadily declining over recent years
3. **Underpayment:** Insurance companies may be underpaying homeowners by at least 30% based on Xactimate pricing
4. **Xactimate's own admission:** They report 50% variation in price from low to high within any given market, with 100% variations "not uncommon"

#### Limitations
- Designed primarily for tract homes
- Does NOT accurately reflect costs for: custom-built homes, historic properties, high-value properties
- Adjuster/contractor proficiency directly affects estimate accuracy
- Missing line items are common (supplements frequently needed)

#### Eve's Xactimate Strategy
- Always compare Xactimate estimates against actual contractor bids
- Flag regional pricing discrepancies between adjacent markets
- Identify commonly missed line items that require supplementation
- Reference RSMeans data as independent pricing validation
- Document when Xactimate pricing falls below actual market costs
- Challenge labor depreciation (improper under FL law per Trinidad v. Florida Peninsula)

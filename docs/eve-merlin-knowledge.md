## 1. Bad Faith Indicators

What Merlin identifies as carrier bad faith conduct:

### Statutory Framework
- **Florida Section 624.1551** (2022): Created barriers requiring a court ruling of contract breach before bad faith suits. The First DCA in *Vo v. Scottsdale Insurance Co.* ruled this cannot retroactively eliminate vested bad faith claims.
- Bad faith claims that vested before 2022 statutory changes remain actionable.
- The 2022 reforms eliminated attorney fee recovery, reduced bad faith liability, shortened claim deadlines, and tilted appraisal/arbitration toward insurers.

### Documented Bad Faith Indicators
1. **Lowball initial offers**: Scottsdale offered $420.64 on a claim later appraised at $34,545.66 (*Vo v. Scottsdale*).
2. **Form denial letters**: Standardized denials that don't address specific claim circumstances. Merlin testified before the FL House Committee about this "delay, deny, and defend" strategy.
3. **Desk adjuster dismissals**: Referring policyholders to adjusters who never investigated claims.
4. **Denial of physically impossible exclusions**: Florida Peninsula denied a third-floor condo claim citing flood damage -- physically impossible.
5. **Zero-payment claim patterns**: 14 FL insurers closed >50% of homeowner claims with zero payment in 2024.
6. **Post-payment clawbacks**: QBE paid $300K+ after Hurricane Ian, then tried to recoup via unjust enrichment (*Saveraid Trust v. QBE Specialty*). Court dismissed with prejudice.
7. **Withholding estimates from policyholders**: TSI Adjusters memo forbidding field adjusters from sharing estimates.
8. **AI-driven denials without human review**: Carriers deploying AI as "silent adjusters" to predict settlements and recommend denials without disclosure.
9. **Aggressive litigation positions against established practices**: Demanding line-item appraisal awards not required by policy language; arguing matching is excluded from commercial policies.
10. **Claims file secrecy**: Refusing to provide claims file materials to policyholders.

### Key Bad Faith Case Law
| Case | Holding |
|------|---------|
| *Vo v. Scottsdale Insurance Co.* | New statutes cannot retroactively eliminate vested bad faith claims |
| *Saveraid Trust v. QBE Specialty* | Insurers cannot use unjust enrichment to claw back deliberate payments |
| *Eddy v. Farmers Property Casualty* (Ohio) | Discovery of attorney-client materials now requires prima facie bad faith showing |

## 2. Carrier Tactics

Documented patterns of carrier misconduct:

### Claim Handling Misconduct
- **Delay, Deny, Defend**: The foundational carrier strategy. Small claim amounts make litigation economically impractical, forcing policyholders to absorb losses.
- **Using unlicensed adjusters**: American Coastal fined $400K; Monarch fined $325K; Sutton National fined $50K.
- **Missing statutory deadlines**: Failing to acknowledge claims within 14 days; missing 90-day payment/denial deadlines.
- **Suppressing estimates**: Claims managers instructing adjusters not to share estimates with policyholders (TSI Adjusters memo). Violates FL Emergency Rule 69BER24-4.

### Valuation Manipulation
- **Xactimate manipulation**: Federal Judge Varholak ordered native Xactimate file production, recognizing that line-by-line estimates can conceal improper alterations. He personally witnessed Xactimate tampering in a prior federal criminal case.
- **Collapsing ACV and RCV**: Public adjusters submitting estimates with identical ACV/RCV and zero depreciation triggers summary judgment (*Mulas v. Westchester Surplus Lines*).
- **Excluding matching costs**: Carriers arguing matching should not be included in ACV calculations. Merlin: "Some Florida insurers should be ashamed."
- **Capping damages at ACV after wrongful denial**: Denying coverage, then arguing damages limited to ACV because repairs weren't completed. Sixth DCA rejected this in *Universal v. Rodriguez*.

### Structural/Systemic Tactics
- **Anti-PA endorsements**: Velocity Risk inserted endorsements prohibiting policyholders from hiring public adjusters. FAPIA/NAPIA sued under FDUTPA, public policy, and antitrust.
- **DOAH arbitration abuse**: Citizens routed disputes to DOAH -- court found "unmistakable and severe" irreparable harm, structural bias, denial of basic procedural protections. Hillsborough County judge halted the scheme as likely unconstitutional.
- **Affiliate profit hiding**: OIR completed a study showing "hundreds of millions of hidden profits" flowing through affiliate companies, MGAs, TPAs, and reinsurers. The report was buried; former Commissioner Altmaier left to become an insurance lobbyist.
- **Surplus lines exploitation**: Velocity operated in surplus lines to bypass regulatory oversight available in admitted market.
- **Regulatory capture**: Six days before receiving a $325K fine, Monarch received regulatory approval to assume 25,000 Citizens policies.
- **AI as silent adjuster**: Allstate CIO acknowledged AI composes claims-related emails with human adjusters merely reviewing. Companies suppress public statements about AI involvement rather than providing transparency.

### Notice Traps
- **The Clark Opinion**: *Homeowners Choice v. Clark* (1D2023-1622, Mar. 2025) -- requires policyholders to provide separate notices for each coverage category, not just general loss notice. Merlin calls this "hyper-technical nonsense."

## 3. PA Best Practices

What Merlin recommends for public adjusters:

### Documentation Standards
1. **Never collapse policy language into industry shorthand**: "Wind-driven rain" doesn't appear in most policies. The actual requirement is proving wind caused a physical breach in the building envelope.
2. **Document the storm-created opening with building-specific proof**: Photographs of specific damage points, contemporaneous observations, expert opinions identifying precise failure locations.
3. **Properly separate ACV and RCV**: Never submit estimates listing identical ACV and RCV with zero depreciation -- courts treat these as replacement-cost-only estimates triggering summary judgment.
4. **Include legitimate depreciation analysis**: Real depreciation evidence with supporting data.
5. **Retain qualified experts early**: Invoices alone are insufficient to prove damages (*Bagelheads v. Indian Harbor*). Expert testimony connecting damage to covered perils is essential.

### Regulatory Compliance (Rule 69BER24-4)
- All estimates must use electronic estimating software with monthly-updated unit-cost pricing.
- Every modification to default calculations requires a variation report with supporting market data.
- Revised estimates must identify what changed, why, and who made changes.
- All estimate versions (original and revised) must be retained.
- Violations constitute breaches of professional conduct -- license suspension/revocation possible.

### Claim Process
- **Treat supplemental claims as fresh adjustment cycles**: Cooperate with EUOs and reinspections. Never refuse an EUO unless criminal implications exist -- "everything to lose and nothing to gain" from refusing (*Treasure Cay v. Frontline*).
- **Understand post-loss duties**: Failure to comply with EUO, reinspection, and document production forfeits claims. Complete refusal triggers presumption of prejudice.
- **Document disagreements in writing**: Before litigation, send detailed letters presenting evidence and analysis of why the insurer's position is incorrect.
- **Be aware of the notice trap**: After *Clark*, ensure initial loss notice itemizes all potential coverage categories.

### Professional Protection
- **Fight anti-PA endorsements**: Velocity Risk endorsements prohibiting PA hiring are being challenged. Florida Supreme Court recognized public adjusting as "valid and legitimate" (1958). FL Administrative Code prohibits insurer-employed adjusters from discouraging PA hiring.
- **Know your fee recovery limits**: PA fees generally cannot be collected under first-party policies, but commercial policies with explicit "claims adjustment expenses" provisions may provide limited recovery (*CSX Corp. case*).
- **Understand fraud accusation risks**: The Ricky McGraw case shows fraud accusations can be loud and dismissals silent. Maintain meticulous records to defend against baseless allegations.

## 4. Policy Interpretation

How to read coverage provisions per Merlin's guidance:

### Replacement Cost Value (RCV)
- **Insurers cannot cap at ACV after wrongful denial**: Once breach occurs, damages are backward-looking. "An insurer cannot deny coverage, deprive the insured of funds needed to repair, and then use the lack of repairs as a shield against full liability." (*Universal v. Rodriguez*, Sixth DCA)
- **Homeowners can claim RCV without completing repairs**: When insurers deny coverage entirely, policy payment timing provisions cannot restrict damages at trial (*Brito v. Citizens*).
- **Loss-settlement clause is a timing mechanism, not a coverage gate**: Initial ACV paid first, then replacement benefits follow as work progresses (*Weston v. Universal*).

### Actual Cash Value (ACV)
- **Glens Falls doctrine**: *Glens Falls Insurance Co. v. Gulf Breeze Cottages* (1949 FL Supreme Court) -- depreciation should NOT apply to partial losses requiring repair. Purpose: restore insured to pre-loss position.
- **Broad Evidence Rule**: Florida permits any evidence logically supporting a reasonable property valuation estimate (*SFR Services v. Tower Hill Prime*). Damages need not achieve mathematical precision.
- **Matching is required for true indemnity**: ACV calculations must include matching costs. Ignoring matching undermines indemnity. NAIC Model Unfair Claims Practice Act acknowledges excluding matching violates proper claims practices.
- **"As determined by us" shifts burden to insurer**: When policy defines ACV with this phrase, insurer must prove depreciation amounts (*SFR Services v. Tower Hill Prime*).
- **Underpayment timing matters**: Filing suit without contemporaneous evidence of underpayment creates litigation risk (*Bailetti v. Universal*). Document disagreement early.

### Material Misrepresentation
- **False applications void coverage entirely**: *Botelho v. Indian Harbor* -- wrong answers on applications constitute material misrepresentation. Post-binding inspections do not cleanse false applications.
- **Coverage never reaches substantive questions**: Once misrepresentation established, summary judgment ends dispute before coverage arguments are heard.

### Wind-Driven Rain
- Policies require proof that wind caused a physical breach in the building envelope through which rain entered.
- Must provide building-specific proof of wind damage -- industry terminology without physical evidence fails.
- *Mulas v. Westchester Surplus Lines Ins. Co.* (M.D. Fla. Jan. 2026) demonstrates these evidentiary requirements.

### Constructive Total Loss
- Buildings can be constructive total losses when government ordinances block repair, even if significant structure remains.
- Two tests: **Loss-of-Identity** (lost essential character) and **Usable-Remnant** (no reasonable owner would repurpose remaining materials).
- When classified as constructive total loss, ordinary "Ordinance or Law" exclusions become inapplicable.
- FL Stat. Section 627.702 (amended post-2004-2005 hurricanes): Total loss must stem from covered peril for face-value recovery under Valued Policy Law.

### Key Interpretive Principles
- Courts must read the FULL policy -- rare COVID victory proved this (*covid-policy-language-coverage-victory*).
- "All evidence and inferences must be viewed in the insureds' favor when testing a directed verdict" (*Weston v. Universal*).
- Factual questions (damage scope, matching, building codes) belong exclusively to juries, not judges.

## 5. Appraisal Strategy

When and how to invoke appraisal:

### When Appraisal Applies
- **Amount-of-loss disputes**: Appraisal resolves disagreements about damage value, not coverage disputes.
- **Supplemental claims as part of initial claim**: If insurer accepted coverage and claim not settled, subsequent damage is part of the same claim -- appraisal eligible (*Heritage v. Wellington Place HOA*).
- **Both parties can invoke**: Insurers can force even small disputes ($2,000) into appraisal (*Can an Insurer Force a $2K Dispute into Appraisal?*).

### Appraisal vs. Arbitration in Florida
- **Appraisal is NOT arbitration**: *Allstate v. Suarez* (833 So.2d 762, Fla. 2002) -- FL Supreme Court held appraisal is an "informal appraisal proceeding, not a formal arbitration hearing." Overruled prior appellate cases treating appraisals as arbitration.
- This distinction affects procedural rules, judicial review standards, and enforcement mechanisms.

### Umpire Selection Strategy
- Umpire reviews two existing appraisals and resolves differences -- does not create independent estimates.
- **Vetting requirements**: Request detailed CVs, check public records and court appointment history, direct contact for transparency verification.
- **Red flags**: Undisclosed close insurer relationships.
- **Challenge procedures**: Courts can intervene when undisclosed conflicts emerge.

### Line-Item Awards
- **Insurers cannot demand line-item appraisals**: *Great Lakes v. Ming & Kwang Development* -- court rejected insurer demands to separate RCV, ACV, direct/indirect damages, and matching costs when policy language doesn't require it.
- Appraisal must follow actual policy terms, not insurer preferences.
- "Appraisal is a contractual mechanism, and courts will enforce it as written rather than adding conditions an insurer might find convenient."

### Strategic Considerations
- Don't condition appraisal cooperation on litigation positions.
- Appraisal bypasses the need for pre-denial court rulings that Section 624.1551 requires for bad faith claims.
- Appraisal awards alone may not satisfy the statutory prerequisite for bad faith suits under current FL law.

## 6. FL Statute Analysis

Merlin's interpretation of key Florida statutes:

### Section 624.1551 (2022 Bad Faith Reform)
- Created barriers requiring court ruling of breach before bad faith suits.
- Appraisal awards alone don't satisfy this requirement.
- Cannot be applied retroactively to vested claims (*Vo v. Scottsdale*).
- Part of broader legislative package that eliminated attorney fee recovery, reduced bad faith liability, shortened deadlines, and tilted appraisal toward insurers.

### Section 627.7011 (Replacement Cost)
- Governs replacement cost claims handling during normal adjustment.
- Becomes irrelevant after wrongful denial -- post-breach damages are backward-looking (*Universal v. Rodriguez*).
- Homeowners can claim RCV even without completing repairs when insurer denies coverage (*Brito v. Citizens*).

### Section 627.702 (Valued Policy Law)
- Amended post-2004-2005 hurricane seasons.
- Total loss must stem from covered peril for automatic face-value recovery.
- Mixed-cause scenarios no longer guarantee full policy limits.

### Section 627.70154 (Appraisal/Dispute Resolution)
- Citizens policyholders lack protections available under this statute to those with private insurers -- violates due process per court ruling blocking DOAH arbitration.

### Emergency Rule 69BER24-4 / Rule 69B-220.201 (Estimating Standards)
- Mandatory electronic estimating software with monthly-updated pricing.
- Variation reports required for any deviation from defaults.
- Change tracking mandatory: what, why, who.
- All versions retained. Violations = license discipline.
- Applies equally to company adjusters, independent adjusters, and public adjusters.

### Florida House Bill 527 (AI Claims Bill)
- Filed by Rep. Hillary Cassel.
- Requires human adjuster for any denial decision (whole or partial).
- Adjusters must independently verify facts, review AI output accuracy, confirm policy lacks coverage, document who decided, when, and why.
- No other state has taken this specific step. NAIC model bulletins lack enforcement power of statutory law.
- Merlin: "AI can whisper in the adjuster's ear, but the machine cannot push the 'no' button on its own."

### NAIC Model Bulletin on AI (2023)
- Requires documented governance programs, bias testing, human oversight, and compliance with unfair practice laws.
- Lacks enforcement power of state statute -- hence need for FL-specific legislation.

### Regulatory Fine Structure
- $600K combined fines (American Coastal $400K, TypTap $150K, Sutton National $50K) represent minimal accountability for billion-dollar companies.
- Fines don't compensate harmed policyholders.
- Fines lack deterrent power given company revenues.
- Merlin: current fines are "a cost of doing business."

## 9. Merlin's Core Philosophy

Recurring themes across all articles:

1. **"An honest person has nothing to hide"** -- Transparency in claims files prevents bad faith.
2. **"Delay, deny, and defend" remains the dominant carrier strategy** -- Small claims make litigation uneconomical.
3. **Policy language controls** -- Courts enforce written terms, not insurer preferences or industry custom.
4. **Indemnity means restoration to pre-loss position** -- "No better, but certainly no worse."
5. **AI must serve policyholders, not just efficiency** -- "Good faith in property insurance claims handling requires more than efficiency."
6. **Documentation wins cases** -- Expert testimony, contemporaneous observations, building-specific proof.
7. **Regulatory fines are theater without legislative reform** -- Current penalties are "a cost of doing business."
8. **Public adjusters are legitimate professionals** -- FL Supreme Court recognized this in 1958; carriers cannot contractually prohibit their use.
9. **Attorneys make a difference** -- "Policyholders should select their counsel wisely and after a diligent search."
10. **Speed cannot substitute for fairness** -- DOAH, AI, and streamlined processes must preserve constitutional protections.

## 1. Negotiation Frameworks

### 1.1 The Documentation-First Framework

Every negotiation is won or lost before the first conversation with the carrier. Build an unassailable file.

**Phase 1: Evidence Assembly**
- Date-stamped photos/video of ALL damage (wide shots + close-ups)
- Moisture readings with calibrated equipment (document serial numbers)
- Thermal imaging where applicable
- Professional corroborative statements (licensed contractors, engineers, architects)
- Historical maintenance records proving pre-loss condition
- Weather data from NOAA/local stations confirming the peril

**Phase 2: Estimate Preparation**
- Write Xactimate estimates using current regional pricing databases
- Line-item format categorized by room/area -- no lump-sum categories
- Add justification notes to every line item likely to be disputed
- Include overhead and profit (O&P) where multiple trades are involved
- Cross-reference against actual contractor bids for market validation

**Phase 3: Demand Package**
- Cover letter summarizing claim, policy provisions, and total demand
- Complete Xactimate estimate with supporting documentation
- Photo log indexed to estimate line items
- Expert reports (engineers, hygienists, etc.)
- Policy excerpts highlighting applicable coverage provisions

### 1.2 The Incremental Concession Framework

Never negotiate against yourself. Follow this sequence:

| Step | Action | Timing |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Submit full demand with documentation | Day 0 |
| 2 | Wait for carrier's written response | 14-30 days |
| 3 | Identify specific line items in dispute | Upon receipt |
| 4 | Provide additional documentation ONLY for disputed items | 7-14 days |
| 5 | Request re-inspection if scope is disputed | Concurrent |
| 6 | Escalate to carrier's supervisor/examiner | If no movement after Step 4 |
| 7 | Invoke appraisal or file Civil Remedy Notice | When negotiation stalls |

**Key Rules:**
- Never reduce your estimate without the carrier providing a written, line-by-line counter
- Every concession from the carrier must be documented in writing
- If the carrier's adjuster makes verbal promises, follow up with written confirmation
- Keep a detailed communication log (date, time, person, content, outcome)

### 1.3 The Three-Column Comparison

Present disputes visually:

| Line Item | PA Estimate | Carrier Estimate | Difference | Justification |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|------------|---------------|
| Roof tear-off | $X,XXX | $X,XXX | $X,XXX | [Photo ref, code requirement] |
| Underlayment | $X,XXX | $0 | $X,XXX | [FL Building Code ref] |
| O&P 20% | $X,XXX | $0 | $X,XXX | [3+ trades involved] |

This format forces the carrier to address each item individually rather than offering a lump-sum "take it or leave it."

### 1.4 Carrier Adjuster Tactic Recognition

Be prepared for these documented carrier tactics:

| Tactic | Description | Counter |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Comparative Estimate** | Carrier brings in preferred contractors with low-ball estimates | Demand credentials, compare against Xactimate pricing, get independent bids |
| **Urgency Pressure** | "Accept now or risk getting nothing" | Document the pressure, respond in writing, cite policy obligations |
| **Pre-existing Damage** | Attribute storm damage to wear/age | Provide maintenance records, expert opinions, date-stamped photos |
| **Scope Minimization** | Acknowledge some damage but undercount affected areas | Demand joint re-inspection, bring your own documentation |
| **Depreciation Abuse** | Over-depreciate to reduce ACV payment | Challenge depreciation schedule with actual condition evidence |
| **Flood Exclusion** | Attribute wind-driven rain damage to flooding | Meteorological data, engineering reports proving wind vs. flood causation |

## 2. Supplement Writing Guide

### 2.1 When to Supplement

- Additional damage discovered during repairs (hidden damage)
- Original scope missed items visible upon closer inspection
- Code upgrades required by building department
- Price increases in materials/labor since original estimate
- Items the carrier's adjuster failed to include in their scope

### 2.2 Florida Supplement Deadlines

| Type | Deadline |
|------|----------|
| Initial/Reopened claim | 1 year from date of loss |
| Supplemental claim (new damage discovered) | 18 months from date of loss |

### 2.3 Supplement Structure Template

```
SUPPLEMENTAL CLAIM SUBMISSION

Claim Number: [XXXXXX]
Policy Number: [XXXXXX]
Date of Loss: [MM/DD/YYYY]
Insured: [Name]
Property Address: [Address]

REASON FOR SUPPLEMENT:
[Concise explanation of why additional funds are needed.
Reference specific areas/items not in original scope.]

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE:
1. Photos (attached, indexed by room/area)
2. Moisture readings / test results
3. Contractor findings during demolition/repair
4. Code requirements from building department
5. Updated material pricing documentation

REVISED XACTIMATE ESTIMATE:
[Attach full revised estimate in line-item format]

LINE-ITEM COMPARISON:
| Item | Original Estimate | Supplement Amount | Total | Reason |
|------|------------------|-------------------|-------|--------|
| [Item 1] | $X,XXX | $X,XXX | $X,XXX | [Reason] |
| [Item 2] | $0 | $X,XXX | $X,XXX | [Hidden damage] |

TOTAL ORIGINAL ESTIMATE: $XX,XXX
TOTAL SUPPLEMENT: $XX,XXX
REVISED TOTAL: $XX,XXX

REQUEST:
We respectfully request [Carrier Name] review the enclosed
supplemental documentation and revised estimate. We are
available for a joint re-inspection at your convenience.

Submitted by: [PA Name, License #]
Date: [MM/DD/YYYY]
```

### 2.4 Supplement Best Practices

1. **Evidence is everything.** Treat every supplement like a courtroom exhibit -- photos, measurements, expert statements.
2. **Use Xactimate.** Write supplements in the same software carriers use. This eliminates format disputes.
3. **Be specific.** "Additional water damage found behind drywall in master bedroom south wall" not "more damage found."
4. **Reference the original.** Show exactly what changed and why with line-item comparisons.
5. **Invite re-inspection.** Proactively offer carrier access to verify claimed damage. This builds credibility.
6. **Follow up.** If no response within 14 days, send written follow-up. Document every attempt.
7. **Preserve evidence.** Photograph hidden damage IN PLACE before remediation. Once demolished, evidence is gone.

## 3. Appraisal Invocation Criteria

### 3.1 When to Invoke Appraisal

Appraisal is appropriate when:

- The dispute is over the **amount of loss** (not coverage)
- Negotiation has reached an impasse after documented good-faith efforts
- The gap between PA estimate and carrier payment exceeds 30-40% of claim value
- The carrier has made their "final" offer and it remains substantially inadequate
- The cost of appraisal (appraiser fee + half the umpire fee) is justified by the potential recovery
- The claim is complex enough that a neutral third party adds value

Appraisal is NOT appropriate when:

- The dispute is about **coverage** (covered vs. excluded peril)
- The carrier has denied the claim entirely
- The amounts in dispute are too small to justify appraiser/umpire costs
- There are bad faith issues better addressed through litigation

### 3.2 Florida Appraisal Process

| Step | Timeline | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Day 0 | Send formal written demand for appraisal per policy language |
| 2 | Within 20 days | Each party selects a competent, impartial appraiser |
| 3 | Within 35 days of demand (or 15 days after appraisers begin selection) | Appraisers agree on a neutral umpire |
| 4 | If umpire not agreed | Either party may petition court for umpire appointment |
| 5 | Appraisal hearing | Each appraiser presents their estimate; umpire resolves differences |
| 6 | Award | Any two of three (appraiser + umpire, or both appraisers) sign binding award |

### 3.3 Appraiser Selection Strategy

- Select an appraiser experienced in the specific loss type (wind, water, fire)
- Verify they have no conflicts of interest with the carrier
- Confirm Xactimate proficiency and ability to defend line items
- Check track record in appraisal proceedings
- Ensure availability within the statutory timeline

### 3.4 Umpire Selection Strategy

**Critical:** The umpire holds decisive power. Two of three must agree for a binding award.

- **Demand true neutrality.** Carrier appraisers often recommend umpires they have relationships with. Challenge any umpire with disproportionate carrier-side appointments.
- **Research candidates.** Review past appraisal awards signed by proposed umpires. Look for patterns of bias.
- **Have counsel present.** If umpire selection goes to court, ensure your attorney is there to advocate for an unbiased appointment.
- **Submit your own list first.** Propose 3-5 qualified candidates and document the carrier's objections to each.
- **Disqualification criteria.** Object to any proposed umpire who: (a) has financial ties to the carrier, (b) regularly serves as the carrier's appraiser, (c) works for a firm that does significant carrier-side work.

### 3.5 Cost Structure

- Each party pays their own appraiser
- Umpire cost is split 50/50
- Typical appraiser fees: $2,000-$10,000+ depending on claim complexity
- Typical umpire fees: $3,000-$15,000+ split between parties
- ROI calculation: If the gap between your estimate and carrier's offer exceeds 2-3x the appraisal cost, it is generally worth pursuing

## 4. Carrier-Specific Counter-Tactics

### 4.1 Common Carrier Delay Tactics

| Tactic | How to Identify | Counter-Strategy |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Repeated document requests** | Carrier asks for same documents multiple times or requests unnecessary paperwork | Send everything certified mail/email with delivery confirmation. Reference prior submissions with dates. |
| **Adjuster rotation** | New adjuster assigned mid-claim, "needs to review from scratch" | Demand continuity in writing. Send complete file to new adjuster with timeline of prior communications. |
| **Inspection scheduling delays** | Carrier takes weeks/months to schedule inspection | Document all scheduling attempts. After 30 days, send written demand citing F.S. 627.70131 (60-day pay/deny requirement). |
| **"Pending further review"** | Indefinite hold with no specific timeline or action items | Send written demand for specific timeline and identify what exactly is under review. |
| **Low-ball then stall** | Make inadequate initial payment, then slow-walk supplements | Accept partial payment under reservation of rights. File supplement with full documentation immediately. |

### 4.2 Common Carrier Deny Tactics

| Tactic | How to Identify | Counter-Strategy |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Pre-existing damage claim** | Carrier attributes covered damage to prior wear, age, or maintenance | Provide maintenance records, prior inspection reports, expert opinions on causation. |
| **Wrong peril attribution** | Wind damage called "flood"; sudden pipe burst called "long-term seepage" | Engineering report establishing causation. Meteorological data for storm claims. |
| **Policy exclusion overreach** | Carrier applies exclusions beyond their actual scope | Analyze exact policy language. Florida interprets ambiguities in favor of the insured. |
| **Cosmetic damage only** | Carrier limits payment to "cosmetic" repairs when structural damage exists | Independent engineering report. Building department documentation of code requirements. |
| **Failure to mitigate** | Carrier claims insured didn't protect property after loss | Document all mitigation efforts with photos, receipts, and timeline. |

### 4.3 Common Carrier Underpay Tactics

| Tactic | How to Identify | Counter-Strategy |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Scope reduction** | Carrier's estimate covers fewer rooms/areas than actually damaged | Joint re-inspection with detailed scope comparison. Photo documentation of every affected area. |
| **Material downgrade** | Carrier prices cheaper materials than what's installed | Document existing materials with photos. Cite matching requirements in policy. |
| **Labor rate suppression** | Carrier uses below-market labor rates | Provide local contractor bids. Reference Xactimate regional pricing data. |
| **O&P denial** | Carrier refuses overhead and profit when multiple trades are needed | Document number of trades required. Reference industry standard 10%/10% O&P. |
| **Excessive depreciation** | Carrier depreciates beyond reasonable useful life calculations | Challenge with actual condition documentation. Cite Florida case law on depreciation methodology. |
| **Code upgrade denial** | Carrier won't pay for building code compliance upgrades | Provide building department requirements. Reference Ordinance or Law coverage in policy. |

### 4.4 Florida Carrier Denial Statistics (2024)

- Nearly 50% of homeowner claims closed without payment (highest in a decade)
- 13% of denied claims resulted in lawsuits
- Post-hurricane claims most vulnerable to delay/deny/underpay patterns
- Common post-hurricane carrier claim: damage was "pre-existing" or caused by flooding (excluded peril)

## 5. Xactimate Dispute Resolution

### 5.1 Commonly Disputed Line Items

**Roofing:**
- Tear-off vs. overlay (carriers prefer overlay; code may require tear-off)
- Ice and water shield / underlayment (carriers often omit or underprice)
- Drip edge replacement
- Ridge vent / ventilation components
- Starter strip and hip/ridge cap shingles
- Roof deck replacement (plywood/OSB)
- Code-required upgrades (FL Building Code often mandates improvements)

**Water Mitigation:**
- Category determination (Cat 1 vs. Cat 2 vs. Cat 3 water)
- Number of dehumidifiers / air movers and duration
- Antimicrobial application
- Content manipulation / move-out charges
- Monitoring visits
- Emergency service / after-hours rates

**Interior:**
- Matching requirements (e.g., flooring must match throughout continuous area)
- Paint -- entire room vs. affected wall only
- Texture matching (knockdown, orange peel, skip trowel)
- Base/crown molding replacement
- Cabinetry refinishing vs. replacement

**General:**
- Overhead and Profit (O&P) -- 10% overhead + 10% profit is industry standard
- General conditions / supervision charges
- Temporary repairs / board-up
- Debris removal
- Permit fees
- Dumpster charges

### 5.2 O&P (Overhead and Profit) Arguments

**When O&P applies:**
- Three or more trades are involved in the repair
- A general contractor is needed to coordinate the work
- The scope of work requires project management/supervision

**Key arguments for O&P inclusion:**
- Xactimate line-item pricing is subcontractor pricing -- it does NOT include GC overhead or profit
- Industry standard is 10% overhead + 10% profit = 20% total
- Job-related direct supervision is NOT included in GC overhead per Xactimate's own methodology
- If the insured must hire a GC to coordinate multiple subs, O&P is a legitimate cost of repair
- Many Florida courts have upheld O&P as a reasonable and necessary expense

**Carrier O&P denial tactics:**
- "The homeowner can coordinate the trades themselves"
- "We only pay O&P when a GC is actually hired"
- "Our estimate already includes O&P in line-item pricing" (it does not)

**Counter:**
- The policy requires the carrier to pay the cost to repair/replace
- A reasonable person would hire a GC for multi-trade projects
- Xactimate's own documentation confirms line items are subcontractor pricing
- Provide GC contracts or bids showing O&P as a separate charge

### 5.3 Xactimate Pricing Disputes

**Problem:** Xactimate pricing is based on median surveys that may not reflect current local market conditions, especially post-disaster.

**Solutions:**
- Obtain 2-3 local contractor bids to demonstrate actual market pricing
- Document material cost increases with supplier invoices
- Reference Xactimate's own regional price list update dates (they may be outdated)
- Use the "price override" function with documented justification
- Cite post-disaster demand surge as a legitimate market factor

### 5.4 Estimate Quality Standards

- Every line item must have a corresponding photo or measurement
- Use room-by-room categorization (no lump sums)
- Include waste factors appropriate to the repair type
- Account for minimum charges on small-area repairs
- Include all Florida Building Code-required upgrades with code references
- Document existing materials accurately for matching requirements

## 6. Post-SB 2A Strategy Adaptations

### 6.1 What Changed

Florida Senate Bill 2-A (2022 Special Session) and subsequent tort reform (HB 837, 2023) fundamentally altered the insurance claims landscape:

| Change | Impact on PAs |
|--------|---------------|
| **One-way attorney fees eliminated** | Policyholders can no longer recover attorney fees from carriers in most property insurance disputes. Carriers have less incentive to settle. |
| **AOB restrictions** | Assignment of Benefits heavily restricted. PAs cannot rely on AOB-driven restoration companies as referral sources. |
| **1-year claim filing deadline** | New/reopened claims must be filed within 1 year of loss (was longer). |
| **18-month supplement deadline** | Supplemental claims must be filed within 18 months of loss. |
| **60-day pay/deny timeline** | Carriers must pay or deny within 60 days (reduced from 90). |
| **PA fee caps** | 10% for emergency-declared claims (first year); 20% for all others. |
| **Bad faith prerequisites tightened** | Must file Civil Remedy Notice and give carrier 60-day cure period. |
| **Comparative negligence** | Modified to 51% bar (relevant for liability-adjacent property claims). |

### 6.2 Adapted PA Strategies

**Pre-litigation leverage is now everything.** Without one-way attorney fees, carriers know policyholders face significant financial risk in litigation. PAs must maximize pre-litigation outcomes.

**Strategy 1: Bulletproof Documentation**
- Every claim file must be litigation-ready from Day 1, even if you never intend to litigate
- Assume the carrier will deny or underpay and prepare accordingly
- Expert reports, engineering studies, and code analyses should be obtained early

**Strategy 2: Appraisal as Primary Dispute Resolution**
- With litigation more expensive and risky for policyholders, appraisal becomes the preferred alternative
- Build relationships with qualified appraisers and umpires
- Develop expertise in presenting appraisal cases effectively
- Appraisal resolves "amount of loss" disputes without triggering litigation costs

**Strategy 3: Strategic Use of Civil Remedy Notices**
- The CRN is now the primary leverage tool for bad faith claims
- File the CRN properly (specific statutory violations, specific facts, specific policy language)
- Give the carrier the full 60-day cure period -- rushing undermines the process
- If the carrier cures (pays the claim), the policyholder wins without litigation
- If the carrier fails to cure, you have established the foundation for a bad faith claim

**Strategy 4: Attorney Collaboration (Selective Litigation)**
- Partner with attorneys who work on contingency for strong cases
- Focus litigation on clear bad faith scenarios where damages exceed attorney costs
- Use attorneys for coverage disputes (appraisal only covers amount of loss)
- Attorney involvement in umpire selection proceedings when appraisal is invoked

**Strategy 5: Speed and Volume**
- The 60-day carrier response timeline cuts both ways -- use it
- File complete claims quickly with full documentation to start the clock
- If the carrier misses the 60-day deadline, document it as potential bad faith evidence
- Process efficiency matters more than ever with tighter fee caps

**Strategy 6: Policyholder Education**
- Educate clients on the new legal landscape upfront
- Set realistic expectations about timelines and potential outcomes
- Explain that litigation is now a last resort, not a routine tool
- Emphasize the importance of their cooperation in documentation

### 6.3 Fee Cap Strategy

| Claim Type | Fee Cap | Strategy |
|------------|---------|----------|
| Emergency (Year 1 of declaration) | 10% | Focus on high-value claims where 10% provides adequate compensation. Volume matters. |
| Emergency (After Year 1) | 20% | Standard rate. Most profitable window for emergency claims. |
| Non-emergency | 20% | Standard rate. Water, fire, mold, and non-declared-storm claims. |

**Key fee rules:**
- Fees apply only to residential property and condo unit owner policies
- Cannot charge on payments received before the PA contract date
- Cannot charge for services not performed
- Fee is calculated on the claim payment, not the estimate

## 7. Bad Faith Documentation Checklist

### 7.1 Florida Bad Faith Statute (F.S. 624.155)

Bad faith exists when an insurer fails to act in good faith in handling a claim. Documentation is essential because the burden of proof is on the policyholder.

### 7.2 Civil Remedy Notice (CRN) Requirements

Before filing a bad faith lawsuit, you MUST file a CRN with the Florida Department of Financial Services:

- [ ] Use the official DFS form (letters, emails, and faxes are NOT sufficient)
- [ ] State the specific statutory provision violated (include exact language)
- [ ] Describe the facts and circumstances giving rise to the violation
- [ ] Name any individuals involved in the violation
- [ ] Reference specific policy language relevant to the violation
- [ ] File electronically with DFS
- [ ] Wait the full 60-day cure period before filing suit
- [ ] If DFS returns for lack of specificity, revise and refile (clock restarts)

### 7.3 Bad Faith Evidence Checklist

Document and preserve evidence of the following carrier behaviors:

**Delay:**
- [ ] Carrier missed 60-day pay/deny deadline (F.S. 627.70131)
- [ ] Carrier delayed inspection scheduling beyond reasonable timeframe
- [ ] Carrier repeatedly requested same documents already provided
- [ ] Carrier rotated adjusters causing restart delays
- [ ] Carrier placed claim in indefinite "pending review" status

**Deny:**
- [ ] Carrier denied claim without adequate investigation
- [ ] Carrier denied based on exclusions that don't apply to the facts
- [ ] Carrier denied without providing specific policy language supporting denial
- [ ] Carrier's denial letter was boilerplate / not claim-specific
- [ ] Carrier denied despite clear evidence of covered loss

**Underpay:**
- [ ] Carrier's estimate significantly below market repair costs (documented with contractor bids)
- [ ] Carrier refused to pay O&P despite multi-trade repairs
- [ ] Carrier used outdated or below-market pricing
- [ ] Carrier applied excessive depreciation
- [ ] Carrier reduced scope without re-inspection or documentation
- [ ] Carrier refused to pay for code-required upgrades

**Process Violations:**
- [ ] Carrier failed to acknowledge claim within 14 days
- [ ] Carrier's payment not accompanied by coverage statement
- [ ] Carrier failed to provide copy of policy upon written request
- [ ] Carrier attempted to settle under one coverage to influence another
- [ ] Carrier made misrepresentations about policy provisions

### 7.4 Communication Log Template

Maintain this log for every claim:

| Date | Time | Method | Person Contacted | Title/Role | Summary | Outcome | Follow-up Needed |
|------|------|--------|-----------------|------------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| | | Phone/Email/Letter | | | | | |

**Rules:**
- Log EVERY communication, no matter how brief
- Save all emails, letters, and written responses
- For phone calls, note the date, time, duration, person, and what was discussed
- Send written follow-up confirming verbal agreements/statements
- Retain voicemails and record call details immediately

## 8. Fee Structure Reference

### 8.1 PA Fee Caps (Florida Statute 626.854)

| Scenario | Maximum Fee |
|----------|------------|
| Emergency-declared claim (within 1 year of declaration) | 10% of claim payment |
| Emergency-declared claim (after 1 year) | 20% of claim payment |
| Non-emergency claim | 20% of claim payment |
| Supplemental claim on existing PA contract | Same cap as original |

### 8.2 Appraisal Cost Estimates

| Component | Typical Range | Who Pays |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| PA-side appraiser | $2,000 - $10,000+ | Policyholder |
| Carrier-side appraiser | $2,000 - $10,000+ | Carrier |
| Umpire | $3,000 - $15,000+ | Split 50/50 |

### 8.3 ROI Decision Matrix

| Claim Gap | Appraisal Cost Estimate | ROI | Recommendation |
|-----------|------------------------|-----|----------------|
| < $5,000 | $3,000-$5,000 | Negative/marginal | Negotiate harder, avoid appraisal |
| $5,000-$15,000 | $3,000-$7,000 | Moderate | Appraisal if negotiation exhausted |
| $15,000-$50,000 | $5,000-$10,000 | Strong | Appraisal recommended |
| > $50,000 | $7,000-$15,000 | Excellent | Appraisal strongly recommended |

## 9. Critical Deadlines

### 9.1 Policyholder Deadlines

| Deadline | Timeframe | Statute |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Report new/reopened claim | 1 year from date of loss | F.S. 627.70132 |
| File supplemental claim | 18 months from date of loss | F.S. 627.70132 |
| Statute of limitations (breach of contract) | 5 years | F.S. 95.11 |

### 9.2 Carrier Deadlines

| Deadline | Timeframe | Statute |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Acknowledge claim | 14 days from notice | F.S. 627.70131 |
| Begin investigation | Promptly upon receipt | F.S. 627.70131 |
| Pay or deny claim | 60 days | F.S. 627.70131 |
| Respond to CRN | 60 days (cure period) | F.S. 624.155 |

### 9.3 Appraisal Deadlines

| Deadline | Timeframe | Source |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Select appraiser | 20 days after appraisal demand received | Policy language (typical) |
| Agree on umpire | 35 days after demand, or 15 days after appraisers begin selection | Policy language (typical) |
| Court petition for umpire | After 15-day umpire selection period expires | Policy language (typical) |
